Bordeaux

Region snapshot

  • Climate: Maritime; Atlantic + Gironde/Garonne/Dordogne moderate temperatures (with occasional frost).
  • Soils: Left Bank often gravel; Right Bank more clay with limestone base.
  • Scale: Huge production region with a long global export history.

Key grapes (and what they contribute)

  • Cabernet Sauvignon: structure; cassis; thrives on gravel.
  • Merlot: softer fruit; plum/chocolate notes; thrives on clay.
  • Cabernet Franc: floral/spice/pepper.
  • Petit Verdot: color + tannin.
  • Malbec: softens blends (historically important).
White grapes
  • Sémillon: body/weight; riper fruit; waxy/creamy texture.
  • Sauvignon Blanc: acidity + aromatics; “backbone” of many whites.
  • Muscadelle: aromatics.

Bordeaux history + 1855 classification

  • 1152: Eleanor of Aquitaine marries Henry II → long British influence and trade.
  • 1600s: Dutch drainage/levies in Médoc → vineyard expansion + ports.
  • 1800s: disease pressure (oidium, phylloxera, downy mildew).
  • 1855 Classification: ranked top châteaux (Médoc/Graves) + Sauternes/Barsac sweet wines for the World Fair—positioning Bordeaux as an elite luxury region.
  • “En primeur” futures buying became a major force (esp. post-1982).
The five First Growths
  • Château Lafite Rothschild (Pauillac)
  • Château Latour (Pauillac)
  • Château Margaux (Margaux)
  • Château Haut-Brion (Pessac-Léognan)
  • Château Mouton Rothschild (Pauillac; added in 1973)

Left Bank vs Right Bank

  • Left Bank (Médoc/Haut-Médoc): Cabernet Sauvignon dominant; gravel; structured/age-worthy; often the most expensive.
  • Right Bank: Merlot dominant with more Cab Franc; clay soils; famous areas include Saint-Émilion and Pomerol (Pétrus).

Graves, Sauternes, Entre-Deux-Mers

  • Graves: red blends + white blends (Sémillon/Sauvignon).
  • Sauternes: sweet wines shaped by botrytis (noble rot); tiny yields; harvested in passes (“tries”).
  • Entre-Deux-Mers: varied soils → varied wines; good value; includes dry whites and sweet-wine areas like Loupiac / Cadillac.
Noble Rot (Botrytis cinerea)
  • Fungus pierces grape skins → moisture loss → concentrated sugar + acid.
  • Hand-harvested in multiple passes; not made every year.
  • Very low yields (e.g., Sauternes max yields are low; d’Yquem famously lower).

Rhône Valley

Northern Rhône

  • Climate: Continental; hot summers, cold winters.
  • Soils: shallow granite + slate.
  • Key grapes: Syrah (reds); Viognier / Marsanne / Roussanne (whites).
The Mistral wind
  • Strong winds reduce cloud cover → more sun/ripening.
  • Helps regulate temperature.
  • Reduces moisture → less rot pressure.
Important Northern Rhône appellations
  • Côte Rôtie: Syrah with small Viognier allowed; steep slopes (“roasted slope”).
  • Condrieu: Viognier; intensely aromatic; sometimes longer skin contact for intensity.
  • Saint-Joseph: mostly Syrah; many east-facing sites → less direct sun.
  • Hermitage: long-lived Syrah; small % white allowed in red by law (traditional practice).
  • Crozes-Hermitage: larger area, often best value.
  • Cornas: Syrah only; south-facing, heat-retaining granite; often the most “gamey.”
  • Saint-Péray: whites (Marsanne/Roussanne); historically important.

Southern Rhône

  • Climate: Mediterranean; hot dry summers, warm wet winters.
  • Soils: clay/limestone/gravel; sometimes large stones (“galets”).
  • Key red grapes: Grenache, Syrah, Mourvèdre, Cinsault, Carignan.
  • Key white grapes: Grenache Blanc, Clairette, Bourboulenc, Roussanne, Marsanne, Viognier, Muscat.
Southern Rhône highlights
  • Gigondas: Grenache-led, rugged and age-worthy; low yields.
  • Vacqueyras: Grenache minimum; approachable, “easy-going.”
  • Rasteau: Grenache-heavy; also known for fortified styles.
  • Châteauneuf-du-Pape: famous blend region; 13 permitted grapes; mostly red, rare whites are coveted.
  • Tavel & Lirac: structured rosé regions (Grenache/Cinsault), higher minimum alcohol.
Côtes du Rhône tiers
  • Côtes du Rhône AOC: broad appellation; large production; blends from both sides of the valley.
  • Côtes du Rhône Villages: stricter rules; often higher alcohol; select villages permitted.

Provence

  • Climate: Mediterranean; lots of sunshine.
  • Soils: limestone and coastal deposits; inland clay + sandy pebbles.
  • Grapes: Grenache, Syrah, Cinsault, Mourvèdre (plus some Cabernet); whites include Clairette, Ugni Blanc, Rolle (Vermentino) and others.
  • Provence is famous for rosé (lifestyle branding), but the region has serious wines beyond rosé.
  • Bandol: Mourvèdre-based age-worthy reds + deep, rich rosés.

Languedoc-Roussillon

  • Climate: Mediterranean with many microclimates; influenced by the Mistral (from the Alps) and sea winds.
  • Soils: clay in plains; limestone and granite in hills.
  • Grapes: Carignan, Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Mourvèdre; whites include Grenache Blanc, Marsanne, Roussanne, Muscat.
  • Long history of quality, but post-phylloxera shifted to bulk/high-yield production (“Le Gros Rouge”).
  • Quality push returned in the 70s/80s; land is cheaper → lots of new producers and a major natural-wine scene.

Tasted wines (from class)

  • Château Cantemerle — Haut-Médoc (2022): Cabernet Sauvignon–led Bordeaux blend (with Merlot, Cab Franc, Petit Verdot).
  • Julien Pilon “Rue des Poissoniers” — Saint-Joseph (2022): Syrah from granite soils; stainless fermentation; aged in barriques (small % new).
  • Jean-Louis Chave “Mon Coeur” — Côtes du Rhône (2023): Grenache/Syrah blend; élevage in a mix of vessels (steel + barrels + larger formats).
  • Fond Cyprès “Premier Jus” (2024): Corbières/Roussillon blend (Carignan + Grenache + a bit of Muscat); part direct press + carbonic/infusion-style elements.
  • Whispering Angel Rosé: Provençal rosé (Grenache/Cinsault + Rolle/Vermentino); rosé as lifestyle brand.

Review: Still Winemaking Process (quick)

  1. Harvest (often at night / cool temps to preserve acidity)
  2. Sorting + destemming
  3. Pressing (free-run + press fractions)
  4. Primary fermentation (reds/rosé often on skins)
  5. Malolactic fermentation (common for reds; sometimes Chardonnay) → softer/creamier feel
  6. Aging:
    • Oak: adds flavor + gentle oxygen exposure
    • Stainless steel: preserves fresh fruit (common for whites/rosé)
    • Cement: “middle ground” vessel
  7. Bottling + bottle settling/aging
