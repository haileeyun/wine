Appellations (sparkling edition)

  • An appellation defines a region + rules to protect traditional techniques.
  • Rules may cover: boundaries, grapes, yield limits, alcohol minimums, aging requirements.
  • Prosecco and Champagne are among the most debated names/regions.
  • Historically, many people used to call any sparkling wine “Champagne” (not allowed now).

Fermentation refresher

  • Yeast + sugar → CO2 + alcohol + heat
  • If CO2 escapes → still wine
  • If CO2 is contained → sparkling wine

Still winemaking refresher (field → bottle)

  1. Harvest (often cool/night picking to preserve acidity)
  2. Sorting + destemming
  3. Pressing (free-run + press fractions)
  4. Primary fermentation (reds/rosé often on skins)
  5. Malolactic fermentation (common for reds; sometimes Chardonnay)
  6. Aging:
    • Oak: adds flavor + oxygen exposure
    • Stainless steel: preserves fresh fruit
    • Cement: “middle ground” vessel
  7. Bottling + bottle settling

Why sparkling wine wants high-acid grapes

  • Sparkling styles favor high acidity (freshness + balance).
  • Acid tends to be higher when grapes are less ripe (often cooler climates).
  • Common sparkling grapes mentioned:
    • Chardonnay
    • Pinot Noir
    • Glera
    • Moscato

How sparkling wine is made (core idea)

After the first fermentation, the wine goes through a second fermentation in a closed vessel so CO2 stays trapped.

Main methods

  • Traditional / Classic Method (in bottle): Champagne, Cava, Franciacorta, etc.
  • Tank Method (Martinotti / Charmat): Prosecco, Lambrusco, Moscato d’Asti, etc.
  • Other: adding CO2 (soda-style) or Pét-Nat (ancestral method)

Traditional / Champagne Method (step-by-step)

  1. Harvest (keep grapes cold for acidity)
  2. Press (white: no skin time; rosé/red: skin contact)
  3. First fermentation
  4. Blending base wines → final cuvée
  5. Bottling + liqueur de tirage (wine/juice + sugar + yeast) → second fermentation in bottle
  6. Aging on lees (yeast sediment) → bready/briochy/toasty notes
  7. Riddling (remuage) to move sediment into the neck
  8. Disgorgement (freeze neck + eject sediment plug)
  9. Dosage / liqueur d’expédition to top up and set sweetness

Dosage sweetness levels (quick reference)

  • Extra Brut / Brut Nature: ~0–5 g/L
  • Brut: ~5–15 g/L
  • Extra Dry (Extra Sec): ~12–20 g/L
  • Sec: ~17–35 g/L
  • Demi-Sec: ~35–50 g/L
  • Doux: > 50 g/L

Tank Method (Martinotti / Charmat)

  1. First fermentation → base wine
  2. Second fermentation happens in pressurized steel tanks with liqueur de tirage
  3. Wine is bottled under pressure

Often chosen to preserve fresh, fruity aromatics (great for Glera/Prosecco and Moscato styles).

Champagne (region)

  • East of Paris (~48th parallel); cold, frost/hail risk
  • “Dual climate”: continental + maritime influence
  • Chalk/limestone soils (classic for high-acid wines)
  • 3 key grapes:
    • Chardonnay: elegance, finesse
    • Pinot Noir: structure, body, power
    • Pinot Meunier: aromatics, freshness
  • Aging rules: minimum ~15 months on lees for NV; ~36 months for vintage (often longer is better)

Quick history highlights

  • Bubbles originally seen as a fault: fermentation stopped in winter and restarted in spring
  • Dom Pérignon: focused on improving viticulture/blending (not actually the one who “invented” bubbles)
  • England played a major role: stronger glass + taste preference helped Champagne scale
  • 1815: Veuve Clicquot popularized riddling (remuage)
  • Major houses established in 1700s (Ruinart, Moët, Roederer, etc.)

Négociants (key term)

  • A négociant buys grapes/wine from growers and produces under their label.
  • Common in Champagne, Burgundy, and Piedmont.

Vintage vs Non-Vintage Champagne

Non-Vintage (NV)

  • Blend across years using reserve wines to maintain a consistent house style
  • Usually shorter aging requirements
  • Often ready to drink on release

Vintage

  • Made only when conditions are good
  • All base wines from one year; longer aging requirements
  • More age potential (and often higher price)

Tête de Cuvée (prestige)

  • Top-tier vintage bottlings in exceptional years (prestige cuvées)
  • Examples mentioned: Pol Roger “Sir Winston Churchill,” Taittinger “Comtes de Champagne,” Roederer “Cristal,” Laurent-Perrier “Grande Siècle,” Moët “Dom Pérignon.”

Other sparkling regions

Italy — Prosecco

  • Veneto / Friuli-Venezia Giulia
  • Top zone: Conegliano Valdobbiadene (DOCG)
  • Grape renamed Glera (2009); appellation now “Prosecco”
  • Tank method preserves fruity/peachy character; usually NV
  • Rosé Prosecco DOC exists (blending in small amounts of red wine before secondary fermentation; allowed from 2020)

Italy — Franciacorta (Metodo Classico)

  • Lombardy; “Italy’s answer to Champagne”
  • Must be traditional method; DOCG (1995)
  • Main grapes: Chardonnay, Pinot Nero, Pinot Bianco
  • Styles/terms:
    • Satèn: white grapes only; slightly lower pressure → creamier mousse
    • Millesimato: vintage
    • Pas Dosé / Pas Opéré: no dosage (like Brut Nature / Extra Brut)
    • Rosé: min 25% Pinot Noir
  • Aging requirements (as noted): ~25 months NV; ~37 months vintage

Italy — Trento DOC

  • Trentino; traditional method required
  • Dolomite influence; production concentrated among a few major producers

Italy — Asti Spumante / Moscato d’Asti

  • Made from Moscato in Piedmont
  • Typically sweet and lower alcohol; tank method
  • Moscato d’Asti is usually softer, lower alcohol (often very low ABV)

Spain — Cava

  • Origin in the 1870s; centered in Catalonia (name relates to “cellar”)
  • Traditional method required
  • Main grapes:
    • Macabeo (Viura): fruit
    • Xarel-lo: strength/body
    • Parellada: softness/aroma
  • Chardonnay sometimes used for body/structure
  • Not confined to one contiguous geographic area (recognized in 1986)

United States (sparkling)

  • No single Champagne-style geographic rule; producers experiment by region
  • Both traditional and tank methods used
  • Cool-climate areas mentioned: Anderson Valley, Carneros, Oregon/Washington, Finger Lakes, Long Island, etc.

    • Aged ~36 months on lees
  • Laherte Frères “Rosé de Meunier” Extra Brut — NV

    • Blend of vinifications: macerated + direct press + still red components
    • Fermented in various sizes of wood barrels
  • Prosecco Superiore “Primario” Extra Dry (Ruggeri) — NV
    • Non-vintage; Glera
    • From Valdobbiadene context; freshness + crowd-pleasing style
    • Varied soils mentioned (clay/limestone/sandstone/morainic)
  • Franciacorta Brut “Cuvée Prestige” (Ca’ del Bosco) — NV
    • Chardonnay + Pinot Nero + Pinot Bianco
    • Each variety vinified separately then blended with reserve wines (~20%)
    • Secondary fermentation in bottle
  • Juvé y Camps Brut Nature “Reserva de la Familia” — 2021
    • Traditional method; ~36 months on lees
    • Parellada + Xarel-lo + Macabeo (from Penedès)
    • Single vintage
  • Moscato d’Asti “La Rosa Selvatica” (Icardi) — 2024
    • Organic; low alcohol
    • Tank method; good with cheese or dessert
