Lecture 3 — Northern France

Theme: Northern France is where climate + law + terroir really show up in the glass—crisp whites, elegant reds, and some of the world’s strictest appellations.

What affects wine-growing regions?

Climate expectations

  • Cool climate: less ripe grapes → lower alcohol; lighter color/pigments; lighter, softer wines.
  • Warm climate: riper grapes → higher alcohol; thicker skins/darker color; juicier, riper fruit flavors.

Appellations (why they exist)

  • An appellation is a legally defined region with rules meant to protect tradition and quality.
  • Rules can cover:
    • Geographic boundaries
    • Allowed grape varieties
    • Yield limits
    • Minimum alcohol
    • Aging requirements
  • Appellations originated in France.

Phylloxera (why modern wine law got serious)

  • Late 1800s / early 1900s: insect attacks vine roots and prevents healing.
  • Solution: graft onto American rootstock (naturally resistant).
  • After recovery, France faced cheap imports + widespread imitation/adulteration → stronger regulations were needed.

French wine law & classification (labels you’ll see)

  • INAO created in 1935.
  • AOC (Appellation d’Origine Contrôlée): official region with strict rules.
  • AOP (EU version): Appellation d’Origine Protégée.
  • IGP / Vin de Pays: more flexible (“country wine”).
  • Vin de France / Vin de Table: least regulated, everyday wine.
  • Grand Cru / Premier Cru: site quality terms (meaning varies by region).
  • Champagne note: Champagne labels don’t need to print AOC/AOP on the bottle.

Alsace

Region snapshot

  • Climate driver: Vosges Mountains (creates a drier “rain shadow” effect).
  • Soils: very diverse (granite, sandstone, volcanic sediments, limestone).
  • Main grapes:
    • Whites: Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Pinot Gris, Muscat, Pinot Blanc, Sylvaner, etc.
    • Red: Pinot Noir

Style + history notes

  • Back-and-forth French/German control affected quality historically.
  • Alsace is often labeled by grape variety (German influence).
  • 51 Grand Cru vineyards; historically focused on “noble grapes” (Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Muscat, Pinot Gris).
  • Chaptalization: adding sugar to must to raise potential alcohol (common in cold regions).

Tasted wine (Alsace)

  • Riesling Grand Cru “Frankstein” (Domaine Weinbach) — 2022
    • Organic/Biodynamic
    • Grand Cru vineyards; granite soils
    • Older vines (~60 years)
    • Hand-picked; light press; fermented in large oak foudre

The Loire Valley

Region snapshot

  • France’s longest river (~1,000 km) and a historic trade route.
  • Climate: maritime in the west → more continental in the east.
  • Soils: stony in the west; schist/clay/tufa farther east.
  • Very diverse styles across subregions.

Subregions (west → east)

  • Pays Nantais
  • Anjou–Saumur
  • Touraine
  • Central Vineyards

Key grapes

  • Whites: Melon de Bourgogne (Muscadet), Chenin Blanc, Sauvignon Blanc, Folle Blanche
  • Reds: Cabernet Franc, Gamay, Pinot Noir, Grolleau, Malbec
Central Vineyards
  • Cool continental climate.
  • Mostly Sauvignon Blanc and some Pinot Noir.
  • Main AOCs: Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé, Pouilly-sur-Loire, Menetou-Salon.
Touraine
  • Tuffeau (tufa) soils; famous châteaux + cellar caves.
  • Cabernet Franc is huge here (locally called Breton).
  • Key reds: Touraine AOC, Bourgueil, Chinon (very known for Cab Franc).
  • Key whites: Vouvray (Chenin) and Montlouis (wide range: dry, sparkling, off-dry, dessert).
Anjou–Saumur
  • Chenin Blanc + Cabernet Franc territory.
  • Savennières: now mostly dry, very age-worthy Chenin.
  • Saumur-Champigny: major Cabernet Franc AOC.
  • Sweet Chenin areas: Coteaux du Layon, Quarts-de-Chaume, Bonnezeaux.
Pays Nantais
  • Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne).
  • Sur lie: aging on lees → extra texture/creaminess (apple/pear notes often discussed).
  • Main AOCs include Muscadet de Sèvre et Maine.

Tasted wine (Loire)

  • Touraine Sauvignon Blanc “Clos de la Grange” (Chidaine) — 2024
    • Stainless steel aging
    • Indigenous yeast fermentation
    • Think: “baby cousin” of Sancerre / Pouilly-Fumé (value-driven, not the same subregion)
  • Chinon “Les Picasses” (Olga Raffault) — 2020
    • 100% Cabernet Franc
    • Older vines (~50 years), mid-slope
    • Chalky clay-limestone soils
    • Long maceration (~25 days); aged in large oak/chestnut foudres

Burgundy

Idea: Burgundy is about place—tiny differences in slope/soil create different wines. “Slope is king.”

Region snapshot

  • Climate: cool continental.
  • Soils: Côte d’Or = limestone/clay/marl; Beaujolais = granite/schist.
  • Main grapes: Pinot Noir + Chardonnay (plus Gamay in Beaujolais, Aligoté as a secondary white).

History + land ownership

  • Monks documented viticulture early (proto-terroir study).
  • French Revolution + Napoleonic inheritance laws → vineyards fragmented into many small holdings.
  • Many growers → strong role for négociants (buy grapes/wine to bottle).

Burgundy quality pyramid (general)

  • Regional (Bourgogne, Mâcon-Villages, etc.)
  • Village (e.g., Mercurey, Pouilly-Fuissé)
  • Premier Cru (specific climats)
  • Grand Cru (tiny top sites; smallest production share)

Main regions of Burgundy (north → south)

  • Chablis: Chardonnay; high acid; Kimmeridgian limestone; frost risk.
  • Côte de Nuits: red-focused; many top Grand Crus; some good value villages too.
  • Côte de Beaune: more white prominence; famous villages (Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet, etc.).
  • Côte Chalonnaise: good value; slower ripening due to exposure; includes Bouzeron (Aligoté), Mercurey, etc.
  • Mâconnais: lots of white; often oak + malolactic styles (“oaky Chardonnay lovers”).
  • Beaujolais: Gamay; carbonic maceration; mostly early-drinking; 10 crus for higher-end wines.

Key grapes notes

  • Chardonnay: apple/pear/citrus; highly responsive to viticulture + winemaking.
  • Aligoté: historically widespread, now smaller + often in less famous sites (Bouzeron is the famous AOC).
  • Pinot Noir: thin skins, high acidity; very site-sensitive; needs good drainage.
  • Gamay: thrives on granite; fruit-forward; central to Beaujolais.

Tasted wines (Burgundy / Beaujolais)

  • Domaine de Montille — Bourgogne Blanc “Le Clos du Chateau” — 2022
    • Clos (walled vineyard); “baby Puligny-Montrachet” vibe
    • Oak-aged
  • Marcel Lapierre — Raisins Gaulois — 2024
    • Beaujolais natural-wine icon (“Gang of Four”)
    • Young vines; short aging; carbonic maceration; native yeasts
    • Fruit from Morgon but declassified due to higher yields
  • Harmand-Geoffroy — Bourgogne Rouge “Les Nouroys” — 2021
    • Producer based in Gevrey-Chambertin (Côte de Nuits)
    • Long, slow fermentation; ~16 months in oak
