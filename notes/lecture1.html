What does “beverage” mean?

Alcohol basics

  • Fermented beverage
  • Yeast feeds on sugar → CO2 + alcohol + heat

Types

  • Wine: fermented grapes
  • Beer: fermented grains
  • Cider: fermented apples
  • Liquor: distilled (can be made from many sources)

Wine basics

  • Made from fermented grapes
  • Old historical origins (wine as safer than water, social/religious uses, etc.)
  • Monks helped document winemaking and vineyard quality early on

Old World vs New World (general)

  • Old World: often less oak, more “elegant” (France, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Germany, Austria)
  • New World: often more oak, riper fruit (US, South America, Australia/NZ, South Africa, etc.)

What affects wine-growing regions?

Climate

  • Cool climate: less ripe grapes → lower alcohol, lighter/softer wines
  • Warm climate: riper grapes → higher alcohol, thicker skins, riper fruit flavors
  • Continental: dramatic seasons; frost/hail risks; more “dramatic” wines
  • Mediterranean: long season, low rainfall in season; often “chill” wines
  • Maritime: water moderates temps; “middle ground”

Soil (high-level)

  • Clay: dense, water-retaining, cool → often bold, deeply colored wines
  • Sand: good drainage, heat retention, phylloxera resistance → often elegant/delicate, lower tannin
  • Limestone: water retention + vine stress → high acidity, tight structure (often mixed with clay)

Sunlight + water

  • Vines need sun (cold regions want more exposure; hot regions may need shade via canopy)
  • Vines need water (too little → irrigation; too much → better drainage / slopes)

Grape species

  • Vitis vinifera: classic European species (most famous grapes)
  • North American species exist and matter historically (rootstocks, some regional styles)

Types of wine (6 main categories)

  • White: juice separated from skins
  • Red: fermented with skins (extended maceration)
  • Rosé: brief skin contact (or blending in some contexts)
  • Sparkling: CO2 trapped from fermentation
  • Dessert: fermentation stopped to keep residual sugar
  • Fortified: boosted with neutral spirits for higher alcohol

Still winemaking (field → bottle)

  1. Harvest (often cool/night picking to preserve acidity)
  2. Sorting + destemming
  3. Pressing (free-run + press fractions)
  4. Fermentation (reds/rosé often ferment on skins)
  5. Malolactic fermentation (common in reds; sometimes Chardonnay)
  6. Aging:
    • Oak: adds flavor + breathes (oxygen exposure)
    • Stainless: preserves fruit; no oxygen
    • Cement: middle ground
  7. Bottling + bottle settling

Vineyard realities

  • Vines take ~3 years to become productive
  • Older vines often produce fewer grapes but higher quality
  • Every year: pruning; sometimes “green harvest”
  • Hazards: frost/hail, disease (phylloxera/mildew), drought

Wine business basics

The Three-Tier System (simplified)

  • Winery → Importer → Distributor → Retail/Restaurant
  • Pricing influenced by: ex-cellar price, exchange rates, shipping/taxes, tariffs, margins

On-premise vs off-premise pricing (rule of thumb)

  • Wine shop shelf: often ~1.5× wholesale
  • Restaurant bottle list: often ~3× wholesale
  • By-the-glass: often ≈ bottle cost per glass program strategy

State-by-state complexity

  • Every state has different laws (NY is strict with posted pricing windows)
  • Some states are control states (extra tiers / higher cost)
  • Shipping + inventory timing: imports can take months; domestic typically faster

Food & wine pairing (rules of thumb)

  • Mirror or contrast (e.g., butter + buttery Chardonnay vs butter + Champagne)
  • Match intensity: bold with bold; delicate with delicate
  • Sweet + salty/spicy works well
  • High fat benefits from tannin (e.g., steak + Cabernet)
  • Sweet with sweet (food shouldn’t overpower wine’s sweetness)
  • Acid with acid (acidic food wants an acidic wine)

Wine tasting: how to improve

  • Taste a lot (repetition builds memory)
  • Steps: sight → swirl → smell → sip → slurp (aerate) → savor/spit
  • What to look for: body/color, aroma, flavor, finish
  • Key sensations: acidity (tingle), alcohol (heat), bitterness, tannin (dry/grip), fruit, and “place” notes (herbs/flint/salt/etc.)

WSET tasting grid (used in class)

  • Appearance: clarity, intensity, color
  • Nose: condition, intensity, aroma characteristics
  • Palate: sweetness, acidity, tannin, body, flavors, length
  • Conclusion: quality (and/or completeness for exams)
