Lecture 5 — Spain & Portugal

Theme: Iberia’s wine identity is shaped by history, climate extremes, and regulation—plus a deep bench of indigenous grapes.

Spain

History (high-level)

  • Roman influence + spread of Latin; agriculture has deep roots.
  • Moors (781–1492): alcohol consumption forbidden but production tolerated → Spain becomes a production hub.
  • Phylloxera in France brought French winemakers to Spain → barrel aging, estate bottling, pruning; American oak became common via trade.
  • Modern appellation system finalized in 1970; innovation accelerates post-1975.

Key context

  • Grapes are Spain’s third-largest crop (after cereals and olives); also used for distillates, concentrates, etc.
  • Low vine density + low average yields; irrigation legalized in 1996 due to heat.
  • Climate ranges from cool/damp NW to hot desert-like SE; many high-altitude vineyards; mountains moderate climate.
  • Soils: limestone widely present; Priorat has distinctive “llicorella” (slate + quartz).

Grapes to know

  • Reds (indigenous): Tempranillo, Garnacha, Monastrell, Graciano, Bobal, Mencía, Cariñena.
  • Whites (indigenous): Viura (Macabeo), Albariño, Verdejo, Palomino, Pedro Ximénez, Xarel-lo, Godello, etc.
  • Airén: hugely planted, drought-resistant; mostly used for brandy; rarely bottled alone.
  • Tempranillo synonyms: Tinto Fino / Tinto del País (Ribera), Tinta de Toro (Toro), Ull de Llebre (Cataluña), Cencibel (La Mancha).

Spanish wine laws

  • DO and DOCa (top tier), plus Vino de la Tierra (VdIT) (similar to IGT).
  • Vinos de Pago: single-estate category independent of DO/DOCa.
  • Aging ladder (general): Joven → Crianza → Reserva → Gran Reserva (some regions are stricter).

Regions & styles (Spain)

Galicia (NW)
  • Cool climate → crisp white wines; key DOs include Rías Baixas and Ribeira Sacra.
  • Rías Baixas: Albariño; fresh/briny; some “barrica” aging.
  • Ribeira Sacra: slate soils; Mencía; often shows herbal/pepper notes.
Castilla y León
  • Continental climate, big temperature swings; the Duero River is central.
  • Ribera del Duero: Tempranillo (Tinto Fino); high elevation/winds; Vega Sicilia is a famous producer.
  • Rueda: Verdejo; crisp/aromatic; sometimes with Sauvignon Blanc; sparkling exists.
  • Toro / Cigales / Bierzo: Toro red focus; Cigales rosado; Bierzo features Mencía and whites like Godello.
North Central Spain: Rioja + Navarra
  • Rioja: DOCa; defines allowed grapes and strict aging structure.
  • Rioja zones: Alavesa (cooler, clay-limestone), Alta (age-worthy; mixed soils), Baja (hottest).
  • Navarra: large region with mixed climates; historically quantity-focused, now more quality.
Catalonia
  • Known for innovation; includes Priorat, Penedès, etc.
  • Priorat: concentrated reds; Garnacha + Cariñena; “llicorella” soils; village category (“Vino de Pueblo”).
Southern Spain
  • 30+ DOs; La Mancha is quantity-driven; Jumilla is Monastrell-based with sandy, phylloxera-resistant soils.

Portugal

History (high-level)

  • Treaty of Windsor (1386) with England → trade foundation for the port industry.
  • Madeira becomes a major fortified-wine production and a key stop for ships.
  • Fortified wine tradition grows; later competition + mildew + phylloxera disrupt (1860s).
  • Modern era: EU admission + political change → higher quality and more diversity from small producers.

Portugal basics

  • ~200 indigenous varieties; major reds include Touriga Nacional/Touriga Franca; whites include Arinto/Alvarinho/Encruzado.
  • Soils: granite/schist (north), limestone (south), plus volcanic/red clay inland.

Portuguese wine laws

  • Revised 2009: DOP / IGP.

Key regions (Portugal)

Vinho Verde
  • Cool, rainy Atlantic climate; youthful (“green”) wines, sometimes slightly fizzy/effervescent.
  • Common grapes: Loureiro, Trajadura, Avesso, Arinto, Alvarinho.
Douro
  • Steep valleys; hot summers/cold winters; many wines built around Touriga Nacional and related grapes.
  • Subregions: Baixo Corgo, Cima Corgo, Douro Superior; much production also goes to Port.
Dão
  • Touriga Nacional + Tinta Roriz common; schist vs granite influences structure vs fruit expression.
Alentejo
  • Hot region; large share of production; easy-drinking reds/whites; Antão Vaz and Aragonez/Tempranillo are notable.

Tasted wines

  • Vinho Verde, Broadbent — NV (Loureiro, Treixadura)
  • Albariño “Dandelion,” Alberto Nanclares — 2023 (organic; lees aging)
  • Ribeira Sacra Mencía “Vel’uveyra,” Ronsel do Sil — 2019
  • Quinta de Lemos “Dona Santana,” Dão — 2008 (blend incl. Touriga Nacional + Tinta Roriz)
  • Viña Cubillo Rioja, López de Heredia — 2017 (Tempranillo; long oak + bottle aging)
  • Priorat “Bellmunt,” Mas d’En Gil — 2021 (organic/biodynamic; Garnacha; slate)
