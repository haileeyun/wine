Lecture 6 — Italy

Theme: Italian wine is “embottlement” of culture: history + cuisine + place.

  • Food-friendly traits: high acidity, minerality, and tannin
  • Diversity: politically one nation, culturally/oenologically “20 different nations”

Core Concepts

  • Cool vs. warm climate: cool → lower alcohol, lighter/softer; warm → higher alcohol, thicker skins, riper fruit.
  • DOC system: rules can control geography, grapes, yield, alcohol, aging, etc.
  • Classification timeline: Vino da Tavola → DOC (1963) → DOCG (1980) → IGT (1992).

Northern Italy

Piedmont

  • Dolcetto: earliest ripening; best young; fresh + fruity (Dogliani highlighted).
  • Barbera: thicker skins, deeper color; medium–full; mostly young, sometimes aged in oak for higher-end styles.
  • Nebbiolo: late ripening (“nebbia” = fog); high tannin + acidity; difficult to grow; tannins can come from seeds + skins.
Barolo (Nebbiolo)
  • 100% Nebbiolo; 11 villages.
  • Soils: Tortonian (younger, magnesium-rich, west) vs. Helvetian (older, iron-rich, east; smaller berries/thicker skins).
  • Aging: min 38 months total; min 18 months in barrel. Riserva aged longer (5 years total; ≥2 in barrel).
Barbaresco (Nebbiolo)
  • Smaller area; slightly cooler/higher elevation than Barolo.
  • Aging: 26 months total; min 9 months in barrel.

Veneto

Valpolicella (Corvina/Corvinone + optional Rondinella/Molinara):

  • Styles: Classico, Superiore, Ripasso
  • Ripasso: wine “re-passed” over skins from Amarone
  • Appassimento: dried grapes concentrate sugar (Recioto retains RS; Amarone fully ferments → powerful/high alcohol)

Friuli

  • Strong white focus; Slovenian/Austro-Hungarian influence
  • Skin-contact tradition; “ponca” soils mentioned
  • Key grapes: Ribolla Gialla, Friulano, Pinot Grigio, Sauvignon; plus some Merlot/Cab blends

Alto Adige

  • German influence; cool climate + minerality → elegant whites
  • Whites: Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Pinot Grigio/Bianco, Kerner
  • Some reds: Lagrein, Schiava, Teroldego

Central Italy

Tuscany

  • Chianti history: long winemaking tradition; Chianti “recipe” historically included Sangiovese + some white grapes, later shifted to all reds.
  • Super Tuscans: started late 60s/70s; international grapes (Cab/Merlot), small barrel aging; often labeled Vdt initially; IGT helped market them.

Brunello di Montalcino

  • Origins linked to selection of Sangiovese Grosso (“Brunello”) and long aging traditions.
  • DOC/DOCG: DOC in 1966; DOCG in 1980 (first in Italy).
  • Rules (high level): release ~5 years after harvest (6 for Riserva), ≥2 years in barrel, 100% Sangiovese, max yield listed in slides.

Southern Italy

Campania

  • Climate/soils: Mediterranean influences + mountains/rivers; volcanic/alluvial soils
  • Whites: Greco (Tufo/tufa), Fiano (Avellino; fruit-forward; historical “bees” story), Falanghina
  • Reds: Aglianico (tannin + acidity; volcanic character; Taurasi DOCG), Piedirosso

Sicily

  • Key grapes listed (Grillo, Catarratto, Nero d’Avola, Nerello Mascalese, etc.)
  • Appellations: Marsala (fortified), Cerasuolo di Vittoria, Etna, Faro
  • Soils: clay/sand + volcanic sediment; climate moderated by mountains, Etna, and winds

Other Mentions

  • Puglia: Primitivo (early ripening; high sugar → high alcohol); rosato tradition
  • Le Marche: Verdicchio (Castelli di Jesi vs Matelica), Lacrima
  • Lambrusco: sparkling via Charmat; great with rich/fatty foods
